The Availability & Prediction Layer
This is the feature that would make FutureFlat feel like magic — and it's the hardest technical challenge in the entire platform.
The question it answers is simple: when will this apartment become available? The answer requires synthesizing signals across multiple data sources into a probabilistic model.
Signal Category 1 — Structural Patterns
Some units turn over predictably based on their nature. Student apartments near ETH and UZH cycle heavily in July–September. Expat-heavy buildings in Seefeld and Enge correlate with corporate relocation cycles — many multinational employees rotate on 2–3 year assignments. Genossenschaft units turn over less frequently but in predictable life-stage patterns (young couples arriving, growing families leaving for suburbs). FutureFlat would model these base rates per building, per unit type, per neighborhood, creating a prior probability of turnover for any given unit in any given quarter — before looking at any specific signals about the current tenant.
Signal Category 2 — Market & Regulatory Signals
Changes in the Referenzzinssatz that enable rent increases can trigger departures. Large employer relocations — if Google expands its Zürich office, demand spikes in Kreis 5 and some current tenants in now-overpriced units elsewhere may move closer. New construction completing nearby creates alternatives that pull tenants away from older stock. ZVV tram line extensions change the commute calculus overnight. Each of these is a detectable, modelable event that shifts turnover probability for affected areas.
Signal Category 3 — Behavioral Micro-Signals
This is the most powerful and most sensitive category. A tenant listing furniture on Tutti or Ricardo. A LinkedIn location change. An uptick in listing views from the IP range associated with a specific building (suggesting a current tenant is browsing alternatives). A Nachmieter post on WG-Zimmer. A Kündigung posted on a community board. None of these are definitive individually, but combined they form a signal. The ethical line is: only publicly available information, never scraped private communications, and always presented as probability rather than certainty.
Signal Category 4 — Direct Inputs
Sometimes the simplest signal is the best. Current tenants who are themselves FutureFlat users and are searching for new apartments are, by definition, likely to vacate. Swiss tenancy law is one of the most tenant-protective legal frameworks in Europe, but almost nobody knows how to use it. The information exists — in the OR (Obligationenrecht), in cantonal regulations, in Schlichtungsbehörde decisions — but it's scattered, legalistic, and practically inaccessible to the average renter. Most tenants don't know they have the right to challenge their rent, don't know how to calculate whether a reduction is justified, don't know the timelines for contesting a Kündigung, and don't know that the Schlichtungsbehörde is free to use. Landlords and Verwaltungen know all of this. The information asymmetry is enormous, and it structurally disadvantages tenants in every interaction.
FutureFlat would close that gap — not by turning tenants into lawyers, but by embedding legal intelligence into every stage of the tenancy lifecycle so that the right information surfaces at the right moment.
Stage 1 — Before You Sign
When you're looking at a unit, FutureFlat would estimate whether the asking rent is legally defensible. Swiss law (OR Art. 269/269a) prohibits excessive rents — specifically, rents that yield an excessive return on the landlord's investment, or rents that are based on a clearly excessive purchase price. In practice, two tests matter: the relative method (comparing to the previous tenant's rent — if the increase exceeds what's justified by the Referenzzinssatz change and cost increases, it's challengeable) and the absolute method (comparing to comparable units in the area — Quartierüblichkeit).
FutureFlat has the data for both. From the registry and historical listings, it knows what the previous tenant likely paid. From the Referenzzinssatz trajectory, it can calculate the legally permissible increase. From its database of comparable units, it can estimate whether the asking rent exceeds the local norm. The output is simple and visible on every unit profile: "This rent appears to be within the legally permissible range" or "This rent is approximately 12% above the estimated permissible level — you may have grounds to challenge it within 30 days of signing."
That 30-day window is critical. Under OR Art. 270, a new tenant can contest the initial rent at the Schlichtungsbehörde within 30 days of moving in, but only if they felt compelled to sign due to personal hardship or market conditions (Notlage or Marktverhältnisse). In Zürich's market, the Notlage argument is strong — and FutureFlat would help tenants understand that before the window closes.
Stage 2 — During the Tenancy
This is where most tenants leave money on the table. The Referenzzinssatz is the key lever. When it drops, tenants have the right to request a rent reduction (Herabsetzungsbegehren) calculated by a specific formula. When it rises, landlords can increase rent, but only following the correct procedure with the correct notice period and the correct form. FutureFlat would track this automatically.
The moment the Referenzzinssatz changes, every tenant on the platform whose rent is tied to a higher reference rate gets a notification: "The Referenzzinssatz has decreased from X% to Y%. Based on your current rent and your lease's reference rate, you are entitled to request a reduction of approximately Z CHF/month. Here's your pre-drafted Herabsetzungsbegehren." One tap to review, one tap to customize, one tap to send — properly formatted, correctly addressed to the Verwaltung, with the right legal citations and the right calculation.
Conversely, when a landlord sends a rent increase notice, FutureFlat would analyze it. "Your landlord is increasing your rent by 150 CHF/month citing the Referenzzinssatz change. Based on the formula, the justified increase is 95 CHF. The remaining 55 CHF appears to be attributed to general cost increases (allgemeine Kostensteigerungen), which requires specific justification. You may want to contest this." It would explain the tenant's options — accept, negotiate, or contest at the Schlichtungsbehörde — with the relevant deadlines and the practical steps for each path.
Beyond rent, the layer would cover Mängel (defects). Swiss tenants have the right to demand repairs and, if defects reduce the unit's usability, to request a proportional rent reduction for the duration. But most tenants don't know how to quantify "proportional" or how to formally report a defect in a way that protects their rights. FutureFlat would provide templates — a Mängelrüge structured correctly, with guidance on what qualifies as a minor versus major defect, and reference to Schlichtungsbehörde precedents on proportional reduction percentages for common issues like Schimmel, heating failures, noise from construction, or broken appliances.
Stage 3 — At the End
Lease termination in Switzerland is heavily regulated and the source of more disputes than any other tenancy issue. FutureFlat would cover both directions.
If you want to leave: the platform knows your lease terms — notice periods, Kündigungstermine (which in Zürich are typically only specific dates per year unless the lease says otherwise), the requirement for written notice received by the landlord before the deadline. It would send you reminders well in advance: "Your next possible Kündigungstermin is September 30. To meet it, your written notice must reach the Verwaltung by August 31 at the latest. We recommend sending it by the 25th via registered mail." It would also help with Nachmieter proposals — under OR Art. 264, you can be released from the lease early if you propose a suitable and solvent replacement tenant, and FutureFlat's pipeline is the perfect mechanism for this.
If you're being terminated: this is where the legal layer is most protective. Swiss law gives tenants the right to contest a Kündigung at the Schlichtungsbehörde within 30 days if it's abusive (missbräuchlich) — for example, retaliatory after you requested repairs or a rent reduction, or during a period of personal hardship. FutureFlat would analyze the context: "You submitted a Herabsetzungsbegehren 3 months ago and have now received a Kündigung. This pattern may constitute a retaliatory termination under OR Art. 271a. You have until [date] to contest." Even when a termination is valid, tenants can request an Erstreckung (extension of the lease) for up to 4 years for residential tenancies, and FutureFlat would explain this right and help prepare the request.
The Schlichtungsbehörde Navigator
For tenants who do need to go to the Schlichtungsbehörde — the free mediation authority that handles most tenancy disputes before they reach court — FutureFlat would serve as a practical guide. What to expect at the hearing, what documents to bring, how the process works, typical outcomes for common dispute types, and estimated timelines based on current caseload data.
